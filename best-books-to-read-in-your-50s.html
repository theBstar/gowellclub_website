The 10 Best Books to Read in Your 50s

September 28, 2024

Entering your 50s can feel like a significant life milestone, often accompanied by changes in career, family dynamics, health, and personal identity. Reading books that resonate with this stage can offer comfort, guidance, and inspiration. Here are some of the best books to read in your 50s, each providing valuable insights for living well during this vibrant phase of life.

1. “The Second Mountain” by David Brooks

David Brooks explores the idea that life consists of two mountains. The first is about personal ambition, career success, and building a stable life. But often, after achieving these goals, people find themselves yearning for something deeper—the second mountain. This mountain is about connection, commitment, and community. Brooks draws on philosophy, psychology, and his own experiences to show how climbing this second mountain leads to a more fulfilling life. For those in their 50s, this book can serve as a guide to finding renewed purpose and joy by focusing on what truly matters—relationships, service, and integrity.

2. “Daring Greatly” by Brené Brown

In "Daring Greatly," Brené Brown delves into the power of vulnerability and how embracing it can lead to a more authentic and wholehearted life. She challenges the societal notion that vulnerability is a weakness and instead shows how it is the key to courage, connection, and meaningful change. This book resonates deeply with those in their 50s who might be reflecting on past experiences and looking to approach the future with greater openness. Brown’s work is a reminder that living boldly and embracing imperfections is crucial at any age, especially as we redefine ourselves in midlife.

3. “The Blue Zones of Happiness” by Dan Buettner

Dan Buettner’s book explores the world’s happiest places and what makes their residents live long, joyful lives. Buettner identifies specific habits, environments, and community practices that contribute to happiness and longevity. From Costa Rica to Denmark to Singapore, he reveals simple yet profound lessons on how to design your life for greater happiness. For those in their 50s, this book offers actionable advice on finding purpose, building connections, and living with joy—key factors that contribute to not just a longer life but a more fulfilling one.

4. “Atomic Habits” by James Clear

James Clear’s "Atomic Habits" is a must-read for anyone looking to make meaningful changes in their life. Clear explains how small, incremental habits can lead to significant improvements over time. The book provides practical strategies for breaking bad habits and establishing good ones by focusing on systems rather than goals. For readers in their 50s, this book is a powerful reminder that it’s never too late to build habits that support health, happiness, and productivity. Clear’s simple, actionable advice helps readers rethink their daily routines and make positive changes, one small step at a time.

5. “Being Mortal” by Atul Gawande

"Being Mortal" by Atul Gawande is a deeply thoughtful exploration of aging, death, and the medical system’s role in the latter stages of life. Gawande, a surgeon, combines medical knowledge with compassionate storytelling to discuss how we can maintain dignity, purpose, and quality of life as we age. He emphasizes the importance of personal choice and honest conversations about end-of-life care. This book is particularly relevant for those in their 50s who are not only considering their own futures but may also be caring for aging parents. It’s a sobering yet inspiring reminder to live fully and prioritize what truly matters.

6. “The Gifts of Imperfection” by Brené Brown

Another impactful book by Brené Brown, "The Gifts of Imperfection," encourages readers to let go of societal expectations and embrace who they truly are. Brown’s guide to living a wholehearted life is filled with practical advice on cultivating self-compassion, embracing vulnerability, and letting go of perfectionism. For those in their 50s, this book is a helpful tool for reflecting on past experiences and embracing the present with authenticity and joy. It reminds readers that life is not about being flawless but about finding happiness in embracing our true selves.

7. “The Road to Character” by David Brooks

In "The Road to Character," David Brooks explores the importance of building inner character and living a life that prioritizes moral depth over external success. He introduces readers to historical and contemporary figures who embody humility, resilience, and dedication to others. Brooks argues that true fulfillment comes from virtues such as kindness, humility, and commitment to something greater than oneself. For readers in their 50s, this book is a profound exploration of what it means to live a good life and a valuable reminder to focus on character development in the second half of life.

8. “A Man Called Ove” by Fredrik Backman

This novel by Fredrik Backman tells the story of Ove, a grumpy but lovable man who finds new purpose in life through unexpected friendships. It’s a heartwarming tale that reminds readers that it’s never too late to change, connect with others, and find joy in everyday moments. Ove’s journey from loneliness to community is both touching and humorous, making it a delightful read for anyone navigating the ups and downs of life in their 50s. It’s a celebration of second chances and the impact of human connection.

9. “The Wisdom of Sundays” by Oprah Winfrey

"The Wisdom of Sundays" is a collection of the most insightful moments from Oprah Winfrey’s Super Soul Sunday interviews. The book features wisdom from thought leaders, spiritual teachers, and writers who share their insights on mindfulness, purpose, and living authentically. With stunning photography and reflections on life’s big questions, this book is perfect for readers in their 50s seeking inspiration and guidance. It’s a beautiful reminder to slow down, reflect, and live a life true to oneself.

10. “Educated” by Tara Westover

Tara Westover’s memoir, "Educated," is a powerful story of resilience, transformation, and the quest for knowledge. Growing up in a strict, survivalist family in rural Idaho, Westover did not attend school until she was 17. Despite this, she went on to earn a Ph.D. from Cambridge University. Her story is an inspiring testament to the power of education, self-determination, and the ability to change one’s life at any age. For readers in their 50s, "Educated" serves as a reminder that it’s never too late to learn, grow, and redefine one’s path.

 

These books provide valuable insights, inspiration, and guidance for those navigating their 50s. Whether you’re looking to find new purpose, embrace vulnerability, or simply enjoy a great story, these reads offer something special. They remind us that this stage of life is not just about growing older but about living richer, fuller lives with every turn of the page.
